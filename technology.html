Technologies

Introduction and Importance

Technologies in early childhood education promote creativity since they allow creating in the digital environment, coding, and expressing yourself in multimedia, which promotes EYLF Outcome 5, which provides children with the ability to communicate effectively (Australian Government Department of Education, 2022). In Australia, this is gearing the children towards a digital society and encouraging ethical, balanced use as per national guidelines. Activities develop problem-solving, teamwork, and creative thinking, and adaptive tools are used to meet the needs of individuals. The combination of technologies will connect the traditional play with modern skills, improving cognitive flexibility, social interactions, and agency and solving the problem of equity in access to all learners (Fielding, 2022; Department of Education, New South Wales, 2023).

Resources, Materials, and Digital Technologies

The materials will be tablets with applications such as ScratchJr to code stories and unplugged materials such as deconstructed toys to explore with their hands. Safe use of technology is informed by Australian eSafety resources (Early Childhood Australia, 2021). Tech-infused art recyclable materials are also multimodal and inclusive of creativity (Fielding, 2022). These tools promote iteration, solving, and collaboration, which is in line with EYLF principles of creative and equitable learning experiences (Australian Government Department of Education, 2022).

Learning Experiences/Opportunities

0–2 Years

  • Sensory tech touch: Interact with color/sound apps, igniting creative exploration (Zero to Three, n.d.).
  • Simple device play: Explore old keyboards, fostering curiosity (NAEYC, n.d.).

2–3 Years

  • Directional coding: Use arrow mats for toy navigation, building sequences (Little Bins for Little Hands, 2024).
  • Digital scribbling: Draw on tablets, expressing ideas (Raising Children Network, 2024).

3–5 Years

  • Animation creation: Make stop-motion with toys, enhancing narratives (No Time For Flash Cards, 2023).
  • Robot pathways: Program Bee-Bot routes, solving problems (Brenneman et al., 2019).

6–8 Years

  • Game design: Invent simple apps on Scratch, innovating stories (Star Early, n.d.).
  • Photo editing: Manipulate images for tales, boosting visuals (Fielding, 2022).

Reflection and Evaluation

The introduction of animation creation to 3-5-year-old children and game design to 6-8-year-old children showed both success and areas to work on. Narrative expression was facilitated by animation activities, which are in line with the schema adaptation of Piaget, as children were able to sequence the events creatively and collaborate with each other (Smith, 2015). Visual tools facilitated inclusivity where the diverse learners could engage fully. But there were some technical problems which interrupted the flow sometimes and the scaffolding was at different levels in different kids. Subsequent sessions are to involve checks of devices and systematic peer assistance to develop independence and enhance creativity involvement (No Time For Flash Cards, 2023).

The game design activities promoted the Torrance concept of originality, and debugging developed persistence, problem-solving, and digital skills in iterative processes (Gajda et al., 2017). Levels of engagement were high, but there were difficulties with inequality of device-sharing and a lack of time to engage in reflective discussion. To deal with these, collective technology plans and team reflection are suggested, with equal participation and greater learning. The integration of EYLF reflective practices will increase the level of creativity, collaboration, and inclusivity (Australian Government Department of Education, 2022; Star Early, n.d.). On the whole, the two activities showed a high potential to promote creativity in conjunction with deliberate planning and equal access to digital resources.
